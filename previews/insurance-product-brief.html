Shipping Insurance — Ship It, Defer It, or Kill It?

Product decision brief · Based on proposal 2026-05-12_shipping-insurance.md · External pricing verified May 2026
TL;DR — Bottom Line Up Front

Defer insurance to post-launch, but fix the phantom toggle today. The current toggle charges customers $2.50 for coverage that is never bound — that's a trust and legal issue that must be resolved before you take another live payment. Hiding the toggle takes ~30 minutes. Shipping real insurance correctly takes 1–3 days of code plus a legal-review gate before you can charge for it. At one-user scale today, there's no revenue upside that justifies the legal complexity pre-launch. Hide the toggle now, ship real insurance in your first post-launch sprint.

Biggest risk of doing nothing: You already have one live label where the customer may have paid for phantom coverage. If that package is lost, you have no claim to file and no coverage to offer. Resolve the phantom toggle today — do not take another live payment with insurance checked.

1 Current State — The Phantom Toggle Problem

⚠
The insurance toggle is currently cosmetic. When a customer checks "Add shipping insurance (+$2.50)" and completes checkout, SendMo collects the $2.50 but never passes the insurance field to EasyPost's /buy endpoint. No coverage is bound. The package ships with only the carrier's default liability (USPS Priority/FedEx/UPS Ground: $100 included automatically — not from us). The customer paid for coverage that does not exist through SendMo.

What the current code does

  • display_price_cents adds 250¢ when the toggle is on (collected at Stripe checkout)
  • The /buy call to EasyPost omits the insurance parameter entirely
  • No insurance_easypost_id is stored — there's nothing to file a claim against
  • The existing insurance_cost_cents column in the admin view reflects what was charged, not any actual coverage

Why this must be fixed before any live payment

  • Charging for a service not delivered = potential fraud / consumer protection issue
  • You have one live label already — if that package is lost, you have no coverage to offer
  • As you scale, this becomes a class action waiting to happen

The 30-minute hotfix (Option A)

  • Hide the insurance toggle in the UI entirely
  • Remove the $2.50 from display_price_cents logic
  • Add a "Coming soon" note if desired
  • No legal review needed — you're just removing a broken feature

Key observation on UPS/FedEx "declared value"

UPS and FedEx use the term "declared value" not "insurance." It's a liability cap, not an insurance policy — the carrier will deny claims for reasons outside their scope (e.g., inadequate packaging). Third-party insurance (EasyPost, InsureShield, XCover) covers more scenarios and is what most shipping platforms resell.

3 Economics — Can SendMo Make Money on Insurance?

Industry claim rates (loss/damage)

  • ~1–4% of packages are lost or damaged in transit (industry range)
  • 3–4% arrive with some form of damage (InsureShield / Opensend data)
  • 1–1.5% of annual shipping spend results in a claim (financial exposure basis)
  • At 32 historical shipments: expected claims = 0–1 in this range

At SendMo's current scale, you will almost certainly have zero claims for months. The risk is a single large claim wiping out a month of insurance revenue.

Reseller margin — what's normal

  • Typical wholesale-to-retail markup on parcel insurance: 30–100% over wholesale cost
  • EasyPost charges you 1% of declared value (min $1.00)
  • Market retail price: $1.00–$1.39 per $100 (Stamps, ShipStation)
  • Normal margin at those prices: $0–$0.39 per $100 — extremely thin
  • Pirate Ship charges $1.00 / $100 — they likely break even or use it as retention, not profit

Insurance at the market rate is barely profitable for a shipping platform. The margin is in volume, not unit economics.

EasyPost cost: 1% of declared value, $1.00 minimum. The question is what SendMo charges the customer.

John's preferred formula ($3 + 0.5%) loses money above $600 declared value. This is a real problem — a single $1,000 insured shipment that gets lost costs SendMo $2.00 out of pocket (you paid EasyPost $10, customer paid you $8). The proposal author's recommendation to switch to $3 + 1.5% keeps margin positive at all declared values, but roughly doubles the user-visible price at $100 declared value ($4.50 vs $3.50). A third option — cap declared value at $500 — is operationally clean but forfeits high-value shippers.

Revenue upside at SendMo's current scale (honest math)

At 32 historical shipments and "one user today," the realistic 6-month insurance revenue is:

  • Assume 50–100 shipments/month at 30% insurance attach rate = 15–30 insured packages/month
  • Average declared value: $150; premium at $3 + 1.5% = $5.25; margin = ~$3.75
  • Monthly insurance revenue: $56–$113. Six-month total: ~$340–$675
  • Single $1,000 claim (EasyPost pays you, you pay customer): ~$10 cost to you

At this scale, insurance is a trust and feature signal, not meaningful revenue. The economics become interesting at thousands of shipments per month.

5 Legal + Regulatory — What You Need to Know

ⓘ
This is not legal advice. The below surfaces the questions you'd need to ask a lawyer — which the proposal correctly identifies as a ~30-minute legal-eagle review, not a multi-week engagement.

EasyPost TOS — Is reselling with markup actually permitted?

EasyPost's Master Customer Agreement broadly prohibits reselling or sublicensing their services to third parties "unless expressly permitted." Critically, the agreement specifically prohibits marking up UPS rates for resale. Whether this prohibition extends to insurance is unclear — the agreement references a "Shipping Insurance Addendum" that is not publicly posted.

The critical question: Before charging customers an insurance markup over EasyPost's 1% wholesale rate, confirm with EasyPost (via your account manager or their legal contact) that this is explicitly permitted in your agreement. This is a one-email check, not a legal project. If it's not permitted, Option B (direct insurer relationship) is the path to margin.

Risk level if you skip this: Medium. EasyPost is unlikely to sue a small customer over insurance markup, but their TOS gives them grounds to terminate your account — which would take down all of SendMo, not just insurance.

Is SendMo an "insurance reseller"?

  • The standard industry posture: EasyPost is the named coverage provider; SendMo facilitates; the markup is a service fee
  • Pirate Ship, Shippo, Stamps.com all operate this way without apparent licensing issues
  • The gray area: California's "Cargo Shippers' Agent" classification requires a $10,000 surety bond to procure cargo insurance on behalf of others. Whether a software platform counts as a "cargo shippers' agent" is the question.
  • NY and TX: similar broker-licensing frameworks; NY is more aggressive in enforcement
  • The structural defense: if EasyPost is named as the coverage provider in your customer-facing copy and claims flow through EasyPost (not SendMo), you are facilitating access to a licensed insurer, not acting as an agent. This is the safer posture.

Claims handling responsibility

  • The proposal correctly identifies that v1 is: customer → claims@sendmo.co → John → EasyPost portal
  • Your disclosure must honestly reflect this: "we submit the claim to our coverage provider and pass the payout through to you"
  • If EasyPost denies a claim, you are the customer's point of contact — not EasyPost. You must then explain the denial and potentially dispute it on their behalf.
  • If you promise "30–60 day resolution" in your TOS and EasyPost takes 90 days, you have a TOS breach on your hands even if EasyPost caused the delay
  • Mitigation: use vague-but-honest language ("typically resolved within 30–60 business days, subject to provider timelines")

The proposal's recommended legal gate

The proposal's author recommendation (and this brief's agreement): ship the code in comp/test mode, gate the real-money charge flip on a one-session legal review. You can validate the technical pipe — EasyPost returns a real insurance_easypost_id, the admin column populates, the claim flow is testable — before any customer pays for it. The legal review only needs to happen before you flip the billing switch. That's the right sequencing.
